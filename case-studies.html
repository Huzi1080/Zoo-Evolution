Ethical Case Studies

Problematic Patterns within Zoos

Elephants in Captivity

Elephants require vast landscapes, social complexity, and the ability to move long distances. In captivity, they often experience joint and foot pain, stress, and abnormal behaviors such as pacing and rocking, which is a sign of stress.

  • Singer: Emphasizes that captivity causes too much suffering, which needs to be justified
  • Regan: Elephants' rights are violated by being held captive
  • Pepper: Zoos tend to ignore their need for privacy and space.

Gorillas & Social Stress

Gorillas, similar to humans, have complex social groups (families), and captivity disrupts their way of life. regardless of zoos trying to improve their exhibits.

Big Cats in Limited Space

Despite improvements, most big cat enclosures remain far smaller than their natural ranges, resulting in stereotypic pacing.

Examples of Positive Ethical Change

Detroit Zoo — Elephant Sanctuary Transfer

Detroit Zoo recognized that elephants cannot live ethically in captivity and voluntarily moved them to a sanctuary.

Lincoln Park Zoo — Welfare Science Programs

Conducts research on stress indicators, environmental enrichment, and species-specific welfare improvements.

San Diego Zoo — Habitat-Based Exhibits

Large, immersive habitats focusing on natural behaviors rather than display aesthetics.

Your Philosophical Argument

While modern zoos have adopted conservation and welfare missions, many structural issues remain unresolved. Through the ethical frameworks of Singer and Regan, it becomes clear that:

Some species cannot ethically be kept in captivity under any modern zoo model.

Proposed Solution: A Sanctuary-Integrated Model

  • Phase out captivity for elephants, great apes, and large carnivores.
  • Redirect funding to wild conservation and sanctuaries.
  • Use VR/AR for education instead of live display.
  • Promote global habitat protection rather than captive breeding cycles.

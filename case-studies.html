Ethical Case Studies

Problematic Patterns within Zoos

Elephants in Captivity

Elephants require vast landscapes, social complexity, and the ability to move long distances. In captivity, they often experience joint and foot pain, stress, and abnormal behaviors such as pacing and rocking, which is a sign of stress.

  • Singer: Emphasizes that captivity causes too much suffering, which needs to be justified
  • Regan: Elephants' rights are violated by being held captive
  • Pepper: Zoos tend to ignore their need for privacy and space.

The Detroit Zoo elephant transfer demonstrates how institutions can prioritize ethics over entertainment.

Gorillas & Social Stress

Gorillas, similar to humans, have complex social groups (families), and captivity disrupts their way of life. regardless of zoos trying to improve their exhibits.

  • Jamieson: captivity sends the message that humans may dominate other beings.
  • Even with improved exhibits, ethical issues remain

Big Cats in Limited Spaces

Despite improvements, most big cat enclosures remain far smaller than their natural habitat, resulting in pacing and lack of freedom.

  • Singer: unnecessary suffering
  • Regan: violation of rights
  • Pepper: lack of agency and choice

Examples of Positive Ethical Change

Detroit Zoo — Elephant Sanctuary Transfer

The Detroit Zoo realized that elephants cannot live ethically in captivity and voluntarily moved them to a sanctuary.

Lincoln Park Zoo — Welfare Science Programs

Their program researches stress indicators, environmental improvements, and specific welfare improvements for certain species.

Lincoln Park Zoo - Conservation Ambassador Board

Promotes conservation without relying on animal captivity, along with building public awareness about local ecosystems.

My Solution

While modern zoos have adopted conservation and welfare missions, there are still many issues that remain unresolved. Through the ethical frameworks of Singer, Regan, Pepper, and Jamieson, it becomes clear that we should relocate certain species (elephants, big cats, and apes) into proper sanctuaries along with designing exhibits that provide privacy and choice.

As some institutions have started introducing welfare and sanctuary programs for wildlife, the core structure of zoos hasn’t really changed—they still rely heavily on publicity, control, and confinement. Even though many zoos in the U.S. have made progress, there’s still a lot left to address.

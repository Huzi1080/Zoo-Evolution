Zoos, Ethics, and The Evolution of Captivity

By: Huzaifa Anis
A philosophical and historical exploration of animal captivity, conservation, and moral responsibility.

Project Overview

This website serves as an interactive case study exploring how zoos have evolved from early menageries built for human entertainment to modern institutions with conservation-focused missions. Despite these changes, ongoing ethical concerns remain regarding the captivity and treatment of intelligent, social animals.

This project investigates the philosophical, historical, and moral questions surrounding zoos and animal welfare—ultimately asking:

Have modern zoos truly become ethical conservation spaces, or do their historical patterns reveal deeper issues that remain unresolved today?

Philosophical Framework

Peter Singer — Utilitarianism & Speciesism

  • Animals can suffer; their suffering matters morally.
  • Captivity is only justified if it reduces overall suffering.
  • Raises questions about whether conservation goals outweigh the harms of captivity.

Tom Regan — Animal Rights & Inherent Value

  • Animals are “subjects-of-a-life” with rights and inherent moral worth.
  • Captivity violates autonomy and restricts natural behavior.
  • Suggests many modern zoo practices remain ethically problematic.

What You Will Find on This Site

  • A historical timeline showing the evolution of zoos.
  • Case studies comparing problematic zoo practices and modern reforms.
  • Philosophical analysis applying Singer and Regan to real-world examples.
  • A proposed model for sanctuary-integrated, ethically responsible animal care.
